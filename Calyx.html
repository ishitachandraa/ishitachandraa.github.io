Concept Development


Final Artifact


Calyx is an interactive display designed to bridge the distance between you and your loved ones through visual and physical cues. This embodiment reacts to digital interactions by utilizing a servo-powered flower mechanism and LED indicators to reflect real-time communication updates, such as incoming and outgoing messages and online presence. The project uses a Python script integrated with Selenium to monitor WhatsApp Web activity and send signals to an Arduino, which then orchestrates movements and LED signals that represent various states of connection. The servo animates the bloom and subtle vibrations of the flower, while LEDs flash or remain lit to signify activity, creating an organic and emotional representation of digital connections.

The logic behind Calyx is deeply personal: it is connected to my mother's contact with the aim to maintain consistent communication despite being on different continents and in different time zones.

  • Daily Reminder: Each day, the flower starts unbloomed, serving as a subtle reminder that I haven't reached out to her yet. With each message I send, the flower gradually blooms, symbolizing the growing connection.
  • Online Presence: Due to our differing schedules, finding time to talk can be difficult. When my mom is online, the flower lights up, signaling that she is available for a conversation. This ensures I can make the most of those rare overlapping moments in our busy days.
  • Exclusive Message Alerts: When I'm studying at my desk without my phone(to avoid distractions), Calyx ensures I am exclusively alerted to my mom's texts. The flower's petals vibrate and the lights flicker, letting me stay aware of her messages without checking my phone constantly.

Project Code

The project consists of two main code files: a Python script and an Arduino INO file. The Python script uses Selenium to interact with WhatsApp Web and detect changes such as online status, unread messages, and new incoming messages from a specified contact. It sends signals to the Arduino through a serial connection, prompting different physical responses in the project embodiment. The Arduino INO file reads these signals to control servo movements and LED indicators, representing the changes in a tangible way.
